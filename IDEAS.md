# Game Ideas Queue

One idea per line, starting with `-`. The factory takes the **first** idea each run, removes it, and builds it. Add your own ideas here anytime — they take priority over researched/invented ones.

Format: `- Name or concept — one line of what it is and (if multiplayer) how peers interact.`

- Tiny Tanks — top-down capture-the-flag in a walled maze, two teams, tanks fire bullets that ricochet off walls several times before expiring (so you bank shots around corners and can kill yourself with your own rebound); pickups drop upgrades like spread shot, homing, mines, ricochet count, and speed. Peers each simulate their own tank and broadcast pose + fire events; bullets are deterministic from (origin, angle, seed) so every client resolves the same bounces via patterns/rng.ts. AI tanks fill empty seats and play the flag objective.
- Micro civilisation (Hammurabi-style) — you rule a tiny city-state one year per turn, splitting a grain stockpile between feeding your people, sowing next year's fields, and buying land at a price that swings each year; harvests, rat infestations and plagues then roll against you and your population grows or starves. The whole game is five numbers and four sliders — depth comes from the compounding, not the UI. Fully playable solo; the multiplayer twist is that 2–4 peers each run their own city on the *same* deterministic weather/price seed via patterns/rng.ts (so nobody got a luckier decade), broadcast their year-end stats to a shared ladder, and can ship surplus grain to a starving neighbour — cooperation that costs you land. Balance is the entire game: simulate P(city survives N years) and the greedy-vs-cautious win rate before shipping, don't eyeball it.
- Zombie horde base defence — build and fortify a base by day (walls, barricades, turrets, traps, scavenger runs for scrap and ammo), then survive an escalating night horde that pathfinds toward whatever you left weakest; every wave gets bigger and the map's scrap gets scarcer, so over-fortifying starves you. Co-op, not versus: 2–4 peers share one base and one economy, split the day between building and scavenging, and a downed player is revivable rather than eliminated. The host sims the horde and pathfinding while peers broadcast pose+actions, with host transfer handing the sim to a survivor mid-wave; AI survivors fill empty seats and default to repairing walls. Tune the wave curve with a sim (P(base falls by wave N) per party size) — the difficulty ramp is the opponent.
- Emberwake — an auto-running spark tears across dissolving skybridges while a wave of dark unmakes the track behind it at an ever-rising floor speed; you swipe left/right to change lane and take forced junctions (miss the turn and you run off the edge), swipe up to leap gaps, down to slide under overhangs, and hold a thumb-reachable SURGE pad to dump momentum into a burst — and the hook is that momentum IS your speed, your health and your score at once, so clipping a hazard doesn't kill you, it bleeds momentum and slows you, while threading hairline near-misses and grabbing light-motes tops it back up, and the instant your speed drops below the closing dark you're unmade; every run is banking clean racing lines and deciding when to blow the bank on a surge to clear an otherwise-impossible super-gap. Solo is endless score-attack on a daily worldwide seed (async global ladder) plus a byte-identical input-replay ghost of your own best run, since track and physics are fully deterministic. Multiplayer is a live ghost race: 2–8 peers run the SAME seeded bridge — every hazard, mote, junction and the collapse-speed curve derived from one host-broadcast seed via patterns/rng.ts so nobody draws an easier course — and being a parallel time-trial, each peer simulates only its own runner and broadcasts a tiny pose ping (distance + lane + momentum) that rivals render as translucent ghosts, furthest-distance or last-spark-standing wins, with the host owning only seed, countdown and the final ladder so there is nothing to desync. Balance is the procedural track and the surge economy, not seat order (a parallel time-trial has no first-player edge): simulate a runner-bot over hundreds of seeds and assert run-length lands in a target window with a near-zero unfair-early-death rate, and that a mixed hoard-and-spend surge policy beats both pure-hoarding and surge-spam, tuning the collapse-ramp coefficient, per-hazard drain, near-miss/mote refill and super-gap frequency until skill, not luck, decides the winner.
- Deepwatch — a silent co-op card game where the crew must surface a bank of deep-sea creatures onto one shared line from shallowest to deepest, but each card shows only a shadowy silhouette sinking in a depth-gauge (no printed number), so you read "how deep is mine, really?" purely off the wordless tension of who hasn't played yet; hold your card as the depth bar creeps and release when it feels like nothing shallower is left below you, and every out-of-order surface costs the team one of a few shared air tanks. The team-saving twist is the sonar ping — a face-down token everyone silently agrees to spend (tap-and-hold in unison) that forces each player to discard their single shallowest card face-up, defusing near-misses — plus a rising-tide timer that shrinks each round so late levels get frantic. Solo-first: you play the human seat while 2–3 AI divers hold seeded hands and hesitate on a human-like curve (release delay scales with the gap to the next-shallowest card in play, jittered by rng.ts) so the choke of "do I go now?" survives single-player. Multiplayer is 2–4 peers: the host deals every hand and the tide from one shared seed via patterns/rng.ts, each peer broadcasts only release-timestamps and ping-holds (never card values), and the host adjudicates ordering, air tanks and sonar against the deterministic deck — with, by design, zero chat or table-talk channel. It's co-op so there's no seat-advantage, but the risk to simulate is the difficulty curve: run hundreds of AI-table games to assert P(clear level N) forms a smooth ramp and that median air-tanks-remaining never trivialises or walls a level, tuning tide speed and hand size as the levers.
- Frostward — a creeping frost called the Rime spreads across a square grid as a deterministic cellular automaton and 2–4 players each guard three Hearths in their corner, playing cards each turn to bend the prevailing Gale, dig warm Thaw-patches and raise Ridge-walls the frost can't climb, or Leap an ember of cold beside a rival's Hearth; after everyone commits, the Rime steps once — freezing the cells in the Gale's forward arc while ridges and thawed tiles block it — and the last player with a lit Hearth wins. Fully solo: play a real match against 1–3 seeded bots that steer the front at your corner and defend theirs, or race a daily-seed survival where an escalating Gale and scheduled whiteouts try to snuff all three of your Hearths, async-laddered against everyone on the identical seed. Multiplayer is P2P via Trystero: the host seeds one shared RNG (patterns/rng.ts) so deck order, board and Hearth spawns are identical everywhere, peers broadcast only their single committed card per turn, and because the CA plus every contested-spread coin-flip is drawn from makeRng(seed, turn, cellIndex) all clients recompute byte-identical frost without ever syncing the board. Competitive, so balance is the whole game: the risk is seat/first-player advantage compounding with a 'steer-it-at-the-nearest-Hearth' opening into a runaway blaze, and the levers are seed-randomized move-resolution order, tunable Hearth spawn distance, a mostly player-driven Gale with a hard cap on random drift, and a trailing-player catch-up — the factory sims hundreds of AI-vs-AI games and asserts P(leader-at-move-N wins) climbs smoothly with no early lock-in, seat win rates sit in 45–55%, and blowouts stay rare before it ships.
- Changeling — real chess, standard pieces and win-by-checkmate, with one rule bolted on: the instant one of your pieces captures, it morphs into the type of whatever it just took, so grabbing a lowly pawn with your queen collapses her to a pawn while ramming a pawn into the enemy queen crowns that pawn a queen — suddenly every exchange is a shape-shift you have to actually want, recapture math inverts, and only the king is immune (it captures and stays royal, so checkmate still means something). Fully fun solo against a shallow minimax AI running greedy material + piece-square search over the morph-aware move generator, and over P2P it's desync-proof because there's no randomness and no hidden info at all — each side broadcasts only the from/to of its move and both clients apply the identical forced morph, so the two boards can never drift. Competitive, so balance is gated: the risk is a snowballing leader plus cheap early queen-snatches on top of White's normal first-move edge, so the factory sims hundreds of AI-vs-AI games to assert the shape of P(leader-at-move-N wins), White's seat win rate near 50% and the blowout rate, with the tuning lever being whether the morph is forced or optional (keep-or-take) plus the AI's internal piece values.
- Turntide — draughts where a jump never kills: leap an adjacent enemy and it flips to your colour Reversi-style and stays put, so the 24 stones never leave the board and every capture swings allegiance rather than thinning the ranks; men crown into kings on the far row but a flipped king loses its crown (no king-farming), and a just-converted stone is "cooling" — immune to being re-jumped until it has sat through one enemy turn — which kills flip-flop wars, while a rout threshold (control 18 of 24 = instant win) ends swingy games decisively instead of drawing. Fully solo: play an alpha-beta AI, chase a fewest-moves rout score-attack, or a daily fixed-position "flip-the-board-in-N" puzzle whose seed also picks who moves first. Because gameplay has zero randomness, 2P peers stay in lockstep by broadcasting only the origin square and jump path each turn — both clients re-derive the identical result (flips, crown-strips, cooling flags) from the same deterministic rules via patterns/net.ts so no board state is ever sent, the host validates legality and holds authority, and patterns/rng.ts only seeds side-assignment and the daily puzzle. Competitive, so balance is the game: the runaway risk is a single long forced jump-chain flipping half the board in one turn, tuned by a per-turn conversion cap (at most K flips), forced-capture on/off, cooling duration and the rout threshold — simulate hundreds of self-play games and assert first-player win rate near 50%, a smooth P(leader-at-move-N wins) with no move-3 cliff, and a low draw/flip-loop rate before shipping.
- Rootbound — a boardless hex-tile duel with no board at all: you and an opponent draw from a hand of six creature types and place them edge-to-edge into one ever-growing shared colony, aiming to fully ring the enemy's immobile Heartroot with occupied cells while a strict "one colony" rule forbids any placement or slide that would split the cluster in two; each unit has its own crawl — the Creeper walks one cell around the rim keeping contact, the Burrower slips through any single-file gap to the far side, the Spindle jumps in a straight line over a line of touching pieces, the Warden pins an adjacent enemy so it can't move, and the Sporeling can only be placed (never moved) but converts the colour of whatever it's dropped beside — and the signature twist is polarity: a creature touching mostly your own pieces moves short and defends, but one reaching into enemy territory moves further and strikes, so you're constantly choosing to nest safe or overextend. Fully solo versus a minimax AI that reads the ring-completion threat, plus a daily async-seed puzzle with a fixed opening colony to solve. RNG-free, so P2P is exact: the host assigns seats and both peers keep identical colony state, each turn broadcast as a single {unit, from, to} action that every client re-simulates through the same legal-move and one-colony flood-fill check, with any tie-broken ordering pinned by a match-start seed via patterns/rng.ts so no board state ever crosses the wire. Competitive, so balance is gated: the risk is first-player tempo plus a possibly dominant Burrower teleporting straight to the enemy core, so the factory sims hundreds of AI-vs-AI games to assert first-player win rate stays near even (levers: the opener must place their Heartroot before drawing a second unit, and a Burrower gap-slip costs that turn's placement) and that ring-surround endings don't collapse into unbreakable stalemate draws.
