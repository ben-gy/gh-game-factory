# Game Ideas Queue

One idea per line, starting with `-`. The factory takes the **first** idea each run, removes it, and builds it. Add your own ideas here anytime — they take priority over researched/invented ones.

Format: `- Name or concept — one line of what it is and (if multiplayer) how peers interact.`

> **Motorsport ideas: no real-world series, team, driver, sponsor or circuit names.** "F1" and
> "Formula One" are registered trademarks of Formula One Licensing BV, as are the team and circuit
> names. Generic open-wheel racing with original naming and liveries only — the mechanics are free,
> the branding is not.

> **Ideas "like <published board game>": take the MECHANIC, never the identity.** Game mechanics are
> not copyrightable and reimplementing one is completely legitimate. The title, the box art, the
> character names, the illustrations, the card text and the component design all are protected. So:
> original name, original procedural art, original flavour text, and no visual quotation of the
> physical product. Naming the source game in this file is fine — it is shorthand for the mechanic —
> but the shipped game must never reference it, and the registry/README must describe the mechanic on
> its own terms rather than as "a clone of X".

- Loudness escalation — one phone passed around a group in public: each player in turn must say the word slightly LOUDER than the last, the phone measures it with an AnalyserNode, and the first to bottle it (or to fail to beat the rising threshold) loses. Nothing is recorded, nothing is understood, nothing is uploaded — it is one number against a moving bar. Needs a keyboard/touch fallback (a nerve-holding bar you have to release at the right moment) for anyone without a mic or who denies permission. See principle #23.

- Physics tower stacking, in the shape of **Tricky Towers** (WeirdBeard) — take the mechanic, not the identity (see the note above: original name, original procedural art, and the shipped game must never reference the source). **The signature rule: the bricks are RIGID BODIES, not grid cells.** Nothing snaps to a grid, nothing ever locks, and no row ever clears — you drop a tetromino-ish brick, it falls with real gravity and friction, and it settles wherever momentum and the pile beneath it leave it: leaning, teetering, sliding off. The whole game is that the tower is *actually* balanced rather than notionally balanced, and you lose by TOPPLING, not by filling a space. **This is genuinely new against `ballast`, and the run should say why in its plan rather than logging it as an expansion:** ballast is a cellular grid where rows clear, the stack is rigid and never moves, and its "tilt" is a rendering + reachability rule; here there is no grid, no clears, the stack itself is the simulation, and the failure condition is a physical collapse. Different sim, different verb, different way to die. **Three modes fall straight out of the shape and are real spread, not dials (principle #14):** *race* — first tower past a height line; *survival* — a fixed number of bricks and you lose one for every brick that slides off the plinth, so a reckless placement costs you the brick as well as the height; *precision* — build UNDER a low ceiling or through a narrow gate, which inverts everything and kills the degenerate wide-flat-base strategy. **Netcode: do NOT attempt lockstep.** Rigid-body physics is not reliably deterministic across machines (float and transcendental differences between CPUs and browsers), so use the parallel same-seed race this factory already runs in `ballast`/`lastlight`/`undercut`: each peer simulates ONLY its own tower and the wire carries just the brick-order seed, a low-rate status ping for the opponent mini-towers, sabotage events, and a final result. Float divergence between peers is then harmless, because nobody ever needs to agree about anybody else's tower — only about the brick order and the outcome. **Sabotage** replaces the source's spells: spend a resource earned by placing well to drop a hazard on a rival (a gust, a greased brick, a momentary extra weight). Per the anti-snowball lesson, aim it at whoever leads rather than adding a targeting UI, and make sure it cannot chain into an unrecoverable collapse. **Engine decision to make explicitly:** a small purpose-built 2D sequential-impulse solver for convex polygons (full control, tiny, easier to keep stable) versus pulling in matter.js (fast to start, ~90KB, springier and harder to tune) — decide it in the plan, and either way pin a fixed timestep with substepping, because a physics game that behaves differently at 60Hz and 120Hz is not shippable. **Balance flags for the sim (principle #18):** (a) the one that decides whether this is a game at all — is the outcome SKILL or a coin flip? Run the same policy on the same seed and confirm it reproduces, then confirm a better policy actually wins more; chaotic physics where a hair's difference in placement cascades into a topple is a slot machine wearing a tower. (b) Does a wide flat base dominate every mode? If it does, precision mode is the answer and the other two need a reason to build tall. (c) Topple frequency — constant collapse is infuriating, never collapsing means the physics is decoration; measure it and target a band. (d) Sabotage must not be able to end a run outright from a stable position. **Controls:** no grid means positioning is CONTINUOUS, so a plain horizontal drag places the brick and a tap rotates — no stepped rail needed and certainly no D-pad (principle #19). **Juice is most of the appeal here:** bricks that visibly wobble and creak before settling, dust on impact, a slow-motion beat when the tower is a degree from going, and the camera easing upward as you climb.

- Unhurried travel game on a one-way road, in the shape of **Tokaido** (Antoine Bauza) — take the mechanic, not the identity (see the note above: original name, original procedural art, no reference to the source). **The signature rule, and the whole reason to build it: the traveller who is FURTHEST BEHIND takes the next turn.** You may advance as far along the road as you like, but every stop you skip past is a turn you hand your rivals — so the tension is entirely "how much do I give up to reach that one spot before someone else takes it", with no combat, no dice and no attacking anyone. Stops are single-occupancy, which is what makes a peaceful game genuinely competitive: taking the spring means nobody else can. Set collection supplies the scoring — a panorama built up in strict sequence over many visits (abandon it halfway and it is worth little), souvenirs that score in sets rather than singly, springs, meals, and a few "most of X" end bonuses so several routes to a win exist. Punctuate the road with mandatory gather points where every traveller regroups and the menu is limited and first-come, which resets the field and gives the game a rhythm of chapters. Deeply suited to this factory: turn-based with tiny state and zero hidden information, so the netcode is LOCKSTEP (peers exchange only "I advanced to stop N"); a linear track renders beautifully as a VERTICAL scroll on a phone; and solo-vs-bots works without modification. Three modes are easy and real: a short road, a long road with a richer stop mix, and a variant where the panoramas are shuffled so the optimal route changes per seed. **Balance flags for the sim (principle #18):** "furthest back moves next" is an unusually strong self-balancing catch-up device, so measure (a) whether rushing ahead is ever correct or is strictly dominated — if a greedy sprinter always loses, the road's whole decision is fake; (b) seat fairness given somebody must start at the front of the queue; (c) that the panorama sets do not create one dominant collection every seed. Art direction: flat layered colour planes and lots of negative space in the spirit of Edo-period woodblock landscape prints — composition only, generated procedurally, no images fetched or embedded.
