## Game Details
- **Name / repo:** Turntide / `ben-gy/turntide`
- **Genre:** board
- **Core loop:** A jump **converts** instead of capturing — the jumped stone stays put and changes colour. Material circulates instead of accumulating, so every jump is a two-stone swing and an early lead is not a won game.
- **Multiplayer:** live P2P, 2 players, **lockstep** (zero randomness + zero hidden info ⇒ peers exchange only `{from,path}`; no board state on the wire). The turn clock is the only host-authoritative thing, so host transfer is state-wise a no-op.
- **Stack / render:** vanilla-ts / DOM
- **Engine:** `@ben-gy/game-engine` **v1.1.0** (net, rematch, turn, lobby, rng, sound, storage, drag, mobile)

## Balance — the sim overruled the design three times
Built first, given the last word (principle #18). 400 seeded AI-vs-AI games per mode.

1. **The flip cap is not a balance lever.** It was the plan's headline lever. Sweeping 2/3/4/5/6 gave *byte-identical* results on both big boards — with forward-only jumps a chain runs out of board after ~3 links, so the cap never binds. Floodmark's pitch ("cap 5 = a ten-stone swing") was copy for a mechanic that never fires. Real cascade lever found by measurement: **men jumping backward** (big chains 2%→8%; the only setting where the cap binds). Confined to the 10×10 — on the 8×8 it took blowouts 33%→63%.
2. **Cooling 2 broke seat fairness in a different mode.** The cooling-0 control arm showed cooling barely moves any outcome metric (real job: damping repetition, 318→250 repeats on Skirmish). And cooling 2 pushed **Skirmish's first-player rate to 66%**. Cooling is now 1 globally, pinned by a test.
3. **A seat bias nearly got designed around — sample size was the finding.** Early: 37% (Tidepool), 43% (Floodmark), and a very plausible story. Then the *same* Skirmish config read 41% / 49% / 54% across three seed families; at n≈140 the 95% CI is ±8. At n=400 replicated across three seed families, Tidepool's 6×6 edge **was** real (39.9 / 40.4 / 40.8%), Skirmish's was not. Six fixes measured; rout, cap, forced-capture and backward-jumps **all failed** (35–43%). Only the full 8×8 worked: **49.2 / 50.3%**, blowouts 49%→22%, draws 2%→1%. **The 6×6 was cut.**

Also measured: **greedy chain-grabbing loses** (0–13% vs a searching bot) — the snowball the design feared does not exist. Held up throughout: the **leader curve is flat and slightly declining** in every mode (Skirmish ~57% at ply 30, ~36% at ply 45).

**Mechanism audits (principle #21)**, zero tolerance over the sim's event stream, audited from outside the sim: cap violations **0**, forced-capture violations **0**, stone-conservation violations **0**.

## Two defects found by gates unit tests cannot see
- **The two player colours were the same shade of grey.** `contrast.test.ts` measured teal vs amber at a **1.06:1 luminance ratio** — the palette's own colour-blind-safety claim was false, and every screenshot looked fine. Also caught the cooling rim at **1.63:1 on amber** (the cue deciding whether a jump is legal — invisible) and the last-move highlight at 1.83:1 (in lockstep P2P, the only signal your peer moved). Fixed: teal darkened to `#1a9086`, frost inverted to a dark rim + a notch shape cue, lastMove lightened.
- **Three of the engine's six signaling relays were impaired at once.** Found only by the live two-peer test: `nostr.wine` answers reads but **rejects writes** ("restricted: sign up…"), `relay.damus.io` was rate-limiting announces, `relay.nostr.band` didn't answer. Peers *announce* over these, so a write-restricted relay is a **dead relay that passes a liveness probe** — my first read-only probe said all nine relays were "ok". The two peers settled on non-overlapping working subsets and never met. Worked around with a game-side `relayUrls` override (`src/main.ts`) and **logged in `EXPANSION_IDEAS.md` under Engine**, so one release can fix the fleet instead of each game forking a list.
